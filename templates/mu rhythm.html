Mu Rhythm

The mu rhythm is a type of brainwave that is commonly observed in the region of the brain called the sensorimotor cortex. It is a low-frequency oscillation that typically occurs in the range of 8-13 Hz, known as alpha frequency band, and is often seen when an individual is at rest or in a relaxed state.

The mu rhythm is thought to reflect the neural activity of the motor cortex when it is not actively engaged in movement. It is often suppressed or desynchronized when a person performs a movement or observes someone else performing a movement, which is known as mu suppression. This phenomenon is thought to reflect the activity of the mirror neuron system, which is involved in the imitation of actions and the understanding of others' intentions.

Research has shown that individuals with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) often exhibit altered patterns of mu rhythm activity compared to typically developing individuals. Specifically, some studies have found that individuals with ASD show reduced mu suppression when observing others' movements, which suggests a deficit in the mirror neuron system. This reduced mu suppression may contribute to difficulties in social communication and interaction that are characteristic of ASD. For example, it may lead to difficulties in imitating others, understanding others' intentions, and forming social bonds.

Next, you would use Sobi to separate the mixed EEG signals into their individual components. One of these components may correspond to the mu rhythm.
